Hey, What Ever Happened to BlockBuster?!


The story of BlockBuster is a fascinating one.

As a kid, visiting my local blockbuster on a Saturday night was one of my favorite family activities. We'd run along the corridors of films, amazed at the variety of choices at our fingertips.

A debate would you usually ensue. Should we choose a comedy, action film, chick flick, or so on. So many good films. So little time.

I loved it. The BlockBuster logo was emblazoned on that store, just like the Korean Grocer next door, and the Dunkin Donuts next door to that. How could I know that the empire would eventually go bankrupt (officially in in 2010).

Only 11 years ago, the company had over 9,000 stores across the world, including the US, Australia, Brazil, Canada, and many more. Blockbuster was a trusted brand in the movie and video game rental market. But, things soon started going eerily wrong.

By 2011, Blockbuster was down to 1,700 stores (forced to shut down a whopping 7,300 stores!), and was sold off to cover its massive debt.

So, what happened?


In short: Blockbuster failed to adapt to our changing world. When technology made it possible for people to download movies, or stream them for free online, Blockbuster stuck its head in the sand. Even today, the top executives of Blockbuster are remembered as “the sleeping giants” whose ignorance led to their downfall.

It's so easy (and enjoyable) to look back now, and say that BlockBuster was stupid and ignorant. But, hey! Let's get back to the time that this all happened. Napster had come out and flagrantly ignored copyright laws by making music available online to all.

And what was Judgement Day for Napster? The top music sharers were prosecuted, and the app was shut down. Pepsi even made a commercial, in conjunction with iTunes to nail home the point that pirating media was a fad of the past.



Netflix Enters The Game


On the other side of the coin, companies that succeeded in forecasting where the Movies and TV markets were heading, made themselves a name, and a fortune. Companies like Netflix, that began offering live-streaming services for a monthly subscription fee, are still prospering today, and continuing to grow.

And here's the funniest part of all: in early 2000, Netflix CEO Reed Hastings offered to sell his new company to Blockbuster for $50 million. Today, Netflix shares alone are worth a combined total of $19.7 billion.

Netflix’s CEO recalls the meeting with Blockbuster like so: “they just about laughed us out of their office. At least initially, they thought we were a very small niche business. Gradually over time, as we grew our market, their thinking evolved but initially they ignored us and that was much to our advantage.”



So, what kind of lesson, can we learn from Blockbuster’s huge downfall? Firstly, we must keep ahead of the game, and on top of new technology. By reading the news, and understanding how our world is rapidly changing, we can better understand how the economy of tomorrow will be disrupted.

It’s not easy to predict where the future is leading us. Sometimes all we can do is put our pants on in the morning, one leg at a time.
